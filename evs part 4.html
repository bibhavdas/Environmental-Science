üå± 1. Ecological Succession

Definition: The development of a new community is known as succession. It involves the change from a blank space to a full-grown ecosystem, or from destruction back to development.
Sere: The sequence of vegetation types that occur during succession is called a sere.

Causes of Succession

  • A. Initial Cause: Climatic factors (wind, fire) or biotic factors (invasion) that create a bare area. Examples: Fire, Flood, Landslides.
  • B. Continuing Cause: Processes like migration, aggregation, and competition that drive the changes.
  • C. Stabilizing Cause: Factors that stabilize the community, primarily Climate.

‚öôÔ∏è 2. The Process of Succession

  1. Nudation: Formation of a bare area (e.g., via Volcanic eruption, landslide, flooding). The basic process.
  2. Invasion: Arrival of reproducing organisms (Pioneers).
    • Dispersal: Seeds/spores reach the area by air/water.
    • Ecesis: Successful establishment (germination, growth).
    • Aggregation: Increase in population number.
  3. Competition: Struggle for space and nutrients (Intraspecific or Interspecific).
  4. Reaction: The environment is modified by the organisms, often becoming unsuitable for existing species and suitable for new invaders.
  5. Climax (Stabilization): The final stage. The community becomes stable and tends to remain in possession of the area. It is generally believed that once climax is attained, the community does not change (though storms/disease can alter it).

üåø 3. Types of Succession

Primary vs. Secondary

  • Primary Succession: Formation of a new community where no life existed before (e.g., Bare rock, Glacier retreat). Pioneers establish first.
  • Secondary Succession: Formation of a new community on previous substrata (soil exists) where life was destroyed (e.g., Fire, Flood). This process is faster.

Other Types

  • Autogenic: Replacement driven by factors within the community.
  • Allogenic: Replacement driven by external factors.
  • Autotrophic / Heterotrophic: Based on early dominance of producers or consumers.
  • Retrogressive: Development goes backward due to destruction (e.g., overgrazing).

Succession Based on Environment

  • Hydrosere: Starts in water.
  • Xerosere: Starts in dry, waterless environment.
  • Lithosere: Starts on rocks.
  • Halosere: Starts in saline environment.

üìú 4. Climax Theory

  • 1. Monoclimax Theory (F.E. Clemens): Within a given region, all land surface is eventually covered by a single type of community. This climax is determined solely by Climate.
  • 2. Polyclimax Theory (Tansley): Climax vegetation consists of numerous types controlled by many factors (soil, moisture, etc.), not just climate.

Short Answer Questions

Define ecological succession.
Short Answer: The development of a new community is known as succession.
Long Answer: Ecological succession is the process of change in the species structure of an ecological community over time. It involves the gradual development of a new community in a bare area or the replacement of an existing one. The sequence of vegetation types that occur during this process is called a sere.
What are pioneer species?
Short Answer: The first group of organisms to establish in a bare area (invaders).
Long Answer: Pioneer species are the hardy species which are the first to colonize previously disrupted or damaged ecosystems, beginning a chain of ecological succession that ultimately leads to a more biodiverse steady-state ecosystem. They are characterized by high adaptability, rapid reproduction, and tolerance to harsh conditions.
List the three main causes of succession.
Short Answer: Initial causes, Continuing causes, and Stabilizing causes.
Long Answer: 1. Initial causes: Climatic or biotic factors (like fire, erosion) that create a bare area. 2. Continuing causes: Processes like migration, aggregation, and competition that drive the succession forward. 3. Stabilizing causes: Factors like climate that stabilize the community into a climax state.
Differentiate between primary and secondary succession.
Short Answer: Primary starts on bare rock (no life); Secondary starts on existing soil (after destruction).
Long Answer: Primary Succession occurs in an area where no community existed before (e.g., bare rock, volcanic lava). It is very slow because soil must be formed first. Secondary Succession occurs in an area where a community previously existed but was destroyed (e.g., forest fire, harvested field). It is faster because the soil and some organic matter are already present.
What is nudation in the context of succession?
Short Answer: The formation of a bare area without life.
Long Answer: Nudation is the first stage of ecological succession. It involves the development of a bare site, which is an area without any form of life. This can be caused by topographic (erosion), climatic (storm, fire), or biotic (human activity) factors.
Give an example of a hydrosere.
Short Answer: Succession starting in a water environment (e.g., a newly formed pond).
What is a climax community?
Short Answer: The final, stable stage of succession that remains in equilibrium with the environment.
Long Answer: A climax community is the final stage of succession, remaining relatively unchanged until destroyed by an event such as fire or human interference. It is in equilibrium with the environment and does not favor the invasion of new species.

Long Answer Questions

Describe the entire process of ecological succession, detailing each stage from nudation to climax.
Long Answer: The process of succession involves several distinct stages:

1. Nudation: The formation of a bare area without life (caused by volcanic eruption, landslide, etc).
2. Invasion: The arrival of reproductive bodies of organisms. It includes:
  • Dispersal: Seeds/spores reach the area.
  • Ecesis: Successful establishment and germination in the new area.
  • Aggregation: Increase in population number.
3. Competition: As numbers increase, organisms compete for limited resources (space, nutrients). This can be intraspecific or interspecific.
4. Reaction: The living organisms modify the environment (e.g., adding humus to soil), often making it less suitable for themselves and more suitable for the next stage of invaders.
5. Stabilization (Climax): The final stage where the community becomes stable, complex, and self-perpetuating, in equilibrium with the regional climate.
Compare and contrast primary and secondary succession, providing a real-world example for each.
Long Answer:

Primary Succession:
  • Start: Begins on a bare area where no life existed (e.g., Bare Rock).
  • Soil: No soil is present initially; it must be created by lichens/weathering.
  • Speed: Very slow process (hundreds/thousands of years).
  • Example: Succession on a cooled lava flow or a newly exposed rock surface after a glacier retreats.
Secondary Succession:
  • Start: Begins in an area where life was destroyed but substratum remains (e.g., Burnt Forest).
  • Soil: Soil is already present, often with seeds/roots.
  • Speed: Relatively fast process.
  • Example: Regrowth of a forest after a fire or on an abandoned farmland.
Explain the concept of climax community. What factors can lead to changes?
Long Answer: The **Climax Community** is the end product of succession. It is a stable, mature, self-maintaining, and self-reproducing community that is in equilibrium with the prevailing climate.

According to the Monoclimax Theory, climate is the sole determinant. However, the community can still change due to:
  • Aging: Natural senescence of dominant vegetation.
  • Catastrophes: Storms, fires, floods (Biotic/Abiotic factors).
  • Diseases: Epidemics affecting dominant species.
  • Human Activity: Deforestation, pollution.
These factors can trigger "Retrogressive Succession."
Discuss the characteristics that make pioneer species successful.
Long Answer: Pioneer species (the "Invaders") possess specific traits allowing them to colonize harsh, bare environments:
  • Exploring Habit: Ability to disperse seeds/spores over long distances to find new habitats.
  • Agile Nature: Rapid growth and establishment.
  • Adaptability: Ability to survive in extreme conditions (high light, low nutrients, low water).
  • Wide Food Choice: Not picky eaters (if animals).
  • Good Breeder: High reproductive rate (r-selected species) to quickly populate the area.
  • Tolerance: Can withstand fluctuating environmental conditions.
Elaborate on the various types of ecological succession based on the initial environment.
Long Answer: Succession is classified based on the nature of the starting environment:
  • Hydrosere: Succession starting in a water body (e.g., pond, lake). It progresses from aquatic plants to marsh, to scrub, and finally to forest.
  • Xerosere: Succession starting in a dry, waterless environment.
  • Lithosere: A specific type of Xerosere starting on bare rocks. Starts with Lichens -> Mosses -> Grasses.
  • Halosere: Succession starting in saline water or soil (e.g., Estuaries, salt marshes).
